The tourism industry is booming with 1.3 billion international visitors in 2019 \cite{UNWTO2024a} and reaching 89\% of
pre-pandeminc levels in the first quater of 2024 \cite{UNWTO2024b}. This has a direct impact on traveling carbon 
footprint, not just on how people get to their destinations but also from their accommodation
\cite{Becken2001, Scott2008}. Energy consumption from hotels and other types of accommodation is one of the most
intensives \cite{Jos2016}, forcasted to double by 2040 \cite{Gossling2015}.

Bed and Breakfast style accomodation, particularly peer-to-peer has had a strong growth driven by the popularity of
online platforms like AirBnB \cite{AirBnB2024a} with over 8 million listings globally \cite{AirBnB2024b}. Is in this
type of accommodation where guests have more autonomy over the use of the facilities, and as a result the amount of 
resources consumed. Nevertheless, less research has been done in this area compared to hotels, despite the potential
\cite{Becken2016, Warren2017}.

The "green" consumer, who prioritizes integrating all aspects of sustainability into their tourism experiences,
represents a completely new market segment that industry players are starting to target \cite{Weaver2005}.

Tourism accommodation providers must ensure that their sustainable initiatives are clear and convincing to achieve high 
levels of guest satisfaction and encourage repeat visits \cite{Rahman2015}.

particularly those that effectively communicate their efforts to customers, with a strong emphasis on keeping them 
informed \cite{Jones2014}.

Previous research has shown that electricity accounts for about 70\% of the total energy consumption in tourist
accomodation \cite{Becken2001, Warren2017}. Water usage is also a concern, with a strong nexus between water and
energy which could be reduced through water conservation as it can be a limited resource in some locations 
\cite{Becken2017, Lam2016}.

Apart from excessive consumption, available resources, in particular water, are under pressure due to extreme droughts
associated with climate change, these kind of events put in danger the funcionality of activities like tourism and 
accomodation \cite{Ziervogel2010}.

In tourism destinations, tourists drive economic growth but also increase the demand for resources. This is especially 
concerning in water-scarce areas like islands and coastal zones \cite{Gossling2015}.

Consequently, tourists can exacerbate existing water challenges and increase water demand as water may be used more 
freely \cite{Gossling2015}

To be able to capture small changes in resource use, utilities have to be monitored at a high frequency \cite{Jos2016}. 
This is where intelligent technologies for monitoring come in, giving both the owner and the guest 'real time' readings
at high resolutions \cite{Warren2018a}.

\subsection{Smart Monitoring and the Internet of Things}

Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) is a system that allows two-way communication between a utility and a customer,
it helps consumers monitor their energy and water consumption in near real-time. They offer a range of benefits, from 
accurate consumer billing, less cost of operation and features like leakage detection \cite{Monks2019}.

\cite{Zaslavsky2013} emphasize that while IoT will generate data at high volumes, variety, and velocity, traditional 
computing frameworks will become a limiting factor. They suggest that these frameworks must be augmented with a 
cloud-based infrastructure capable of adapting to fluctuating load demands to provide the necessary elasticity. The need
for speed and storage capacities to keep up with the rapid growth of data generated by "things" highlights the
connection between IoT and Big Data.

However, much of the data generated by IoT remains unused, indicating a significant untapped opportunity
\cite{Manyika2015}.

The industry's environmental impact is multifaceted, encompassing CO2 emissions, food demand, water and electricity
usage, waste disposal, construction activity, and land use. Various IoT applications are emerging to tackle these areas,
with an estimated potential to achieve savings of over 10 percent \cite{Kratzert2020}.

Energy management is already a leading area for IoT investment \cite{Naimat2017} and should be of particular interest to 
the hospitality industry, considering its resource-intensive operations and the frequently resource-constrained 
environments in which hotels are located \cite{Styles2014}.

The potential of IoT in water conservation remains largely untapped, despite the increasing number of water-stressed 
destinations \cite{Tortella2011}. Luxury hotel guests use between 200 and 1,000 liters of water per day, far 
exceeding the consumption of the local population in the same area \cite{Becken2014}.

"In summary, the IoT uses prevalent in hospitality and their interaction with Big Data outline the potential for a 
disruptive shift toward a new era of enterprise architecture and optimized decision-making for the industry"
\cite{Nadkarni2020}.
