The tourism industry is experiencing unprecedented growth, with 1.3 billion international visitors recorded in 2019 and 
reaching 89\% of pre-pandemic levels in the first quarter of 2024 \cite{UNWTO2024a, UNWTO2024b}. This surge in tourism 
has a direct impact on the global carbon footprint, not only through the means of travel but also via 
accommodation-related energy consumption \cite{Becken2001, Scott2008}. Research highlights that the energy consumption 
within the accommodation sector is particularly intensive, with forecasts predicting a doubling by 2040 
\cite{Jos2016, Gossling2015}. Notably, electricity alone accounts for approximately 70\% of the total energy used in 
tourist accommodations \cite{Becken2001, Warren2017}.

Water usage in tourist accommodations also presents significant challenges, particularly given the close relationship 
between water and energy consumption. Water conservation efforts are crucial in regions where water is a scarce resource 
\cite{Becken2017, Lam2016}. Furthermore, extreme droughts, which are increasingly associated with climate change, 
threaten the availability of water resources essential for tourism and accommodation operations \cite{Ziervogel2010}. 
This situation is particularly pressing in water-scarce areas like islands and coastal zones, where tourism drives 
economic growth but simultaneously escalates the demand for already limited resources \cite{Gossling2015}.

As climate change continues to exacerbate water scarcity, it is vital to investigate whether adaptive measures can 
mitigate or exacerbate water inequalities among local populations and tourists \cite{Ziervogel2010}. However, 
influencing guest behavior to promote resource conservation remains a significant challenge, especially given the 
difficulty in monitoring guest consumption patterns in real-time \cite{Juvan2014}.

The rise of peer-to-peer accommodations, particularly through platforms like Airbnb, which boasts over 8 million 
listings worldwide \cite{AirBnB2024a, AirBnB2024b}, presents an alternative model where guests have greater autonomy 
over resource usage. Despite this growing trend, research in this area is limited compared to studies focused on hotels, 
highlighting a gap in understanding the environmental impact of this accommodation type \cite{Becken2016, Warren2017}. 

Interestingly, Airbnb has been identified as promoting a more efficient use of existing resources, positioning itself 
as an environmentally sustainable alternative to traditional hotels. For instance, properties listed on Airbnb are 
reported to use 63\% less energy per guest night in North America and 78\% less in Europe compared to traditional hotels 
\cite{Simon2014}. Additionally, Airbnb home-share properties use 12\% less water per guest night in North America and 
48\% less in Europe \cite{Simon2014}, contrasting sharply with the hotel industry's trend toward decreasing efficiency, 
where energy use per room night has doubled since the 1960s \cite{Prairie2012}.

Airbnb's commitment to sustainability is further demonstrated by the fact that nearly 83\% of its North American hosts 
own at least one energy-efficient appliance \cite{Simon2014}. Moreover, Airbnb's rating system allows for the 
identification of wasteful guests, a feature that is less emphasized in hotels where enforcement of resource 
conservation is minimal \cite{Webb2014}. Additionally, research conducted by Airbnb China in 2018 concluded that Chinese 
tourists are increasingly making environmentally conscious choices in their accommodations, influenced by Airbnb's 
promotion of sustainable listings \cite{AirBnB2018}.

The growth of the "green" consumer market segment, which prioritizes sustainability in their tourism experiences, is 
indicative of a broader shift within the industry \cite{Weaver2005}. Tourism accommodation providers must clearly 
communicate their sustainability initiatives to ensure high levels of guest satisfaction and encourage repeat visits, 
with a particular focus on keeping customers informed about their efforts \cite{Rahman2015, Jones2014}.

By providing clear insights into resource consumption, this project seeks to promote more sustainable practices and 
encourage responsible usage among guests and hosts in shared accommodations made available through online platforms like 
AirBnB.

The growing concern over resource consumption in short-term rental accommodations, particularly in self-catering 
settings like those provided by platforms such as Airbnb, has been increasingly highlighted in recent research. One of 
the primary issues is the lack of awareness among guests regarding their real-time consumption of resources such as 
electricity and water during their stays. Studies have shown that when guests are made aware of their daily consumption, 
significant reductions can be achieved. For example, \cite{Warren2018b} demonstrated a 27.4\% reduction in electricity 
consumption and a 21.6\% decrease in water usage when guests were informed about their consumption from previous days. 
These findings underscore the potential for behavioral change when feedback mechanisms are implemented.

Moreover, the impact of feedback on water usage has been further corroborated by \cite{Stewart2013}, who found that 
individuals could reduce their shower time by 27\% when provided with real-time feedback. This suggests that immediate 
access to consumption data not only increases awareness but also actively influences behavior, leading to more 
sustainable resource use. However, the challenge remains that many guests are not provided with this information in a 
timely or accessible manner, particularly in self-catering accommodations where resource usage is often left 
unmonitored. \cite{Juvan2014} emphasizes this gap, noting that guests' unawareness of their real-time resource use 
poses a significant concern for sustainable tourism.

To address this problem, it is crucial to implement intelligent monitoring technologies capable of capturing small 
changes in resource use at high frequencies. According to \cite{Jos2016}, high-frequency monitoring is essential to 
detect and respond to variations in consumption patterns effectively. Such technologies can provide both hosts and 
guests with real-time, high-resolution readings of electricity and water usage, thereby facilitating more informed 
decisions and promoting conservation. \cite{Warren2018a} further highlights the importance of these technologies in 
enhancing both guest and host awareness of resource consumption, leading to more sustainable practices.

The benefits of implementing such monitoring systems extend beyond resource conservation. For instance, as extreme 
drought events become more frequent, the availability of water is a growing concern for Airbnb hosts. 
\cite{Prinsloo2019} notes that hosts are particularly worried about future water scarcity and its potential impact on 
their businesses. By adapting to these challenges and reducing water consumption, both hosts and guests can contribute 
to mitigating the effects of water shortages. Additionally, monitoring systems may address issues related to indoor 
comfort, such as guests experiencing poor sleep due to uncomfortable temperatures. \cite{Pallesen2016} suggests that by 
providing real-time data on indoor climate conditions, these systems can help optimize comfort levels, enhancing the 
overall guest experience.

In conclusion, the integration of intelligent monitoring technologies in short-term rental accommodations presents a 
promising solution to the challenges of resource consumption. By making guests and hosts aware of their real-time usage, 
significant reductions in electricity and water consumption can be achieved, contributing to more sustainable and 
resilient tourism practices.

\subsection{Shared Economy Systems}

Shared economy is defined as “a socioeconomic system that enables an intermediated set of exchanges of goods and
services between individuals/organizations which aim to increase efficiency and optimization of underutilized resources
in society” \cite{Munoz2017}.

Airbed and Breakfast or Airbnb was founded in 2008 by Brian Chesky, Joe Gebbia, and Nathan Blecharczyk. The idea was to 
rent out an air mattress in their living room to help pay their rent. It has since grown to be a global platform with 
over 4 million listings in 191 countries. The company has been valued at over 100 billion dollars and revolutionized the 
way people travel, stay, and experience new places \cite{Nadkarni2020}.

Sharing economies are based on the idea of sharing goods and services, which can lead to a more sustainable use of 
resources. Airbnb is a prime example of a sharing economy platform that has the potential to promote more sustainable 
ways to travel specially when is a home-sharing accommodation \cite{Webb2014} by reducing the footprint of the stay.

Airbnb introduced its Sustainable Hosting Plan, which includes a £1 million fund (in the United Kingdom) to help hosts 
improve energy efficiency, reduce carbon emissions, and save on their bills, addressing the rising cost of living. 
Partnering with Energy Saving Trust \cite{EST}, Airbnb will offer all hosts a personalized home 
energy efficiency check and action plan, detailing steps to enhance their home's efficiency. Hosts interested in making 
improvements will be connected to local installers and can receive grants of £1,000 or £3,000. Additionally, last year, 
Airbnb set a goal to become a net zero company by 2030 \cite{Airbnb2022}.

\subsection{Smart Monitoring and the Internet of Things}

Smart home technology as we know it today started back in the 1970's with the creation of the X10 protocol
\cite{Caflisch2023}. Lights and appliances were controlled over existing electrical wiring, but with limited and 
unreliable functionality. In the next decade the technology matured but it was not until the 1990's, with the creation 
of the internet that these systems started to be more useful. Users were now able to interact with their homes through 
web interfaces or mobile apps.

This also allowed monitoring the usage of energy, water and gas in real-time which attracted people with an interest in 
reducing their utilities bills. In fact "smart home technology has increasingly focused on energy efficiency and 
sustainability, with devices designed to optimize energy usage and reduce environmental impact" \cite{Caflisch2023}. Now 
people can set energy goals for individual devices or your entire home, measured by day, dollars, or watts. 
Additionally, you can add individual sensors to large energy consumers or pool pumps for more precise monitoring. 

The idea behind this is to enhance energy literacy and encourage behavior changes that lower electricity consumption by 
offering instant feedback on electricity usage \cite{Webb2014}. One home energy monitor company, Sense, estimates that 
their average user saves about 9\% on electric bills \cite{Walker2023}.

There is limited empirical research on IoT deployment in the hospitality industry. Although IoT as a technology concept 
has existed for nearly a decade \cite{Atzori2010}, its viable application in hospitality, especially for generating 
data for analytics and optimization, is a more recent development.

The industry's environmental impact is multifaceted, encompassing CO2 emissions, food demand, water and electricity
usage, waste disposal, construction activity, and land use. Various IoT applications are emerging to tackle these areas,
with an estimated potential to achieve savings of over 10 percent \cite{Kratzert2020}.

Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) is a system that allows two-way communication between a utility and a customer,
it helps consumers monitor their energy and water consumption in near real-time. They offer a range of benefits, from 
accurate consumer billing, less cost of operation and features like leakage detection \cite{Monks2019}.

Energy management is already a leading area for IoT investment \cite{Naimat2017} and should be of particular interest to 
the hospitality industry, considering its resource-intensive operations and the frequently resource-constrained 
environments in which hotels are located \cite{Styles2014}.

The potential of IoT in water conservation remains largely untapped, despite the increasing number of water-stressed 
destinations \cite{Tortella2011}. Luxury hotel guests use between 200 and 1,000 liters of water per day, far 
exceeding the consumption of the local population in the same area \cite{Becken2014}.

However, much of the data generated by IoT remains unused, indicating a significant untapped opportunity
\cite{Manyika2015}.

"the IoT uses prevalent in hospitality and their interaction with Big Data outline the potential for a 
disruptive shift toward a new era of enterprise architecture and optimized decision-making for the industry"
\cite{Nadkarni2020}.

\cite{Zaslavsky2013} emphasize that while IoT will generate data at high volumes, variety, and velocity, traditional 
computing frameworks will become a limiting factor. They suggest that these frameworks must be augmented with a 
cloud-based infrastructure capable of adapting to fluctuating load demands to provide the necessary elasticity. The need
for speed and storage capacities to keep up with the rapid growth of data generated by "things" highlights the
connection between IoT and Big Data.
